## The Gates of Morning: Book 1, Chapter 1 - The Canoe Builder

Dick perched on a coral ledge, eyes scanning the southern horizon.  Behind him, the ocean roared, a furious symphony of crashing waves and wind-blown spray.  But before him, the Karolin lagoon lay serene – a vast, sapphire expanse alive with the cries of fishing gulls.  This tranquil inland sea, ringed by forty miles of coral, was *his*.  He’d only arrived yesterday, but already it felt like his own.

The beach buzzed with activity: women, children, young men – his people – all going about their day. They fished, played, tended their taro patches.  Their canoes rested on the sand, and the empty houses that once housed war canoes were now his as well.

His gaze shifted from the lagoon to the canoe houses, a frown etching itself onto his brow. He turned, facing the relentless breakers and the northern sea.  Invisible beyond the horizon, lay Palm Tree Island – a paradise in name only, a place teeming with savage danger.

Little Tari, son of Le Taioi the net maker, looked up.  Tari was still young, but he understood what the men of Karolin knew: war had swept away their men, leaving the women, children, and boys defenseless, leaderless.  Then, yesterday, a strange boat had arrived from the north, bearing a man – Dick – and Katafa, a woman lost at sea years ago.  The women whispered that this man, this Taori as they called him, was sent by the gods to be their chief.

Tari, unburdened by adult anxieties, blurted out his simple question:  “Taori, who *are* you?”

Dick couldn't answer truthfully.  The name "Dick Lestrange" felt like a distant dream.  His past, before Katafa, was shrouded in a hazy fog.  He could have told Tari the story: of his childhood on Marua (Palm Tree), of Kearney, the old sailor who’d raised him, of their escape from brutal Melanesian invaders who’d burned their ship.  But the memories were fragmented, half-forgotten.  His past only truly began with Katafa.

He looked down at the curious child. “I am Taori, Tari. Why do you ask?”

“I don’t know,” Tari replied, already distracted by children hauling in a net.  "I just ask."

Dick's gaze returned to the north. Tari’s question had stirred a potent mix of memories. He saw again the schooner, burning under the Melanesians’ attack;  Katafa and himself escaping in Kearney's old dinghy.  He saw their arrival on this beach and the villagers' awe. He saw himself, a man deemed a god-sent leader.

But the idyllic image was marred by the memory of those men, the savages he'd fled.  There was no immediate threat – the Palm Tree islanders lacked canoes – but they would build them.  They would see the glint of the Karolin lagoon's reflection in the sky, just as he had.  They would come.  It might take time, but they would come.

Dick was essentially a native, yet the vestiges of his white heritage remained. He could strategize, envision the future. That's why he'd sent a canoe to fetch Aioma, Palia, and Tafata – old men skilled in canoe building.  That's why his eyes lit up at the sight of nearly a hundred young men nearing fighting age.  Underlying everything, fueling his daring, was his love for Katafa – a love threatened, however vaguely.

War canoes... Was he planning to defend the lagoon, or launch a preemptive strike?  He wasn't sure.

A hand landed on his shoulder.  Katafa.  A strand of her dark hair, freed from its vine binding, danced in the breeze. Her luminous eyes were fixed on the north, where he’d been gazing.

“Look,” she breathed.

Through a meteorological quirk, Palm Tree, normally hidden beyond the horizon, was visible – a mirage. It appeared like a stain on the horizon, slowly resolving into a breathtaking island paradise.

Shoulder to shoulder, they stood silently, their untutored minds unaware of the science behind the illusion.  All they saw were the monstrous figures of their attackers on the beach, the wind in their sails as they'd fled, the terrifying cries of the Melanesians, drunk and frenzied.  The beautiful mirage was a stark reminder of the horrors it concealed.

Dick’s nostrils flared, his eyes darkening with hate. They had threatened *him* – that was nothing.  They had threatened *Katafa* – that was everything. And they still threatened her.

Some day, they would come.  The vision of Palm Tree confirmed what his instincts already whispered: they would build canoes, they would see the lagoon's reflection, and they would come.  Those men had no women – and Karolin had plenty.  The islanders' thirst for women, just like his own for Katafa, would drive them.  The threat wasn't just from the north; it lurked wherever greed and hatred roamed.

For Dick, the world consisted only of the water that separated the two islands. There was no Europe, no America, no vast history beyond his own short life and Katafa's. Yet, even in this limited context, he'd learned to fear men, to understand the primal engine of history – mankind’s instinct for war, conquest, and destruction.

The mirage of Palm Tree slowly faded. As they turned from the sea towards the lagoon, Katafa pointed to a canoe approaching from the south – the canoe bearing the canoe builders.  They left the coral and walked towards the beach to meet it.
