## The Gates of Morning: Book 1, Chapter 1 - The Canoe Builder

Dick perched on a coral ledge, his gaze fixed on the southern horizon.  Behind him, the ocean roared, a furious symphony of crashing waves and wind-whipped spray.  But before him, the Karolin lagoon lay serene—a vast, sapphire expanse dotted with fishing gulls, calm as a mirror.  This breathtaking lagoon, ringed by forty miles of coral, was his.  His, since *yesterday*.

The beach buzzed with activity: women, children, and young people toiled in the relentless sun, fishing, playing, tending their taro patches.  They were *his* people now. The canoes pulled up on the sand, the empty houses that once housed war canoes—all his.

His gaze shifted from the tranquil lagoon to the deserted canoe sheds. A frown creased his brow.  He turned his back to the placid water, facing the wild, untamed northern sea. Beyond the horizon, invisible but ever-present, lay Palm Tree Island—a paradise in his mind, but a devil's lair in reality.

Little Tari, Le Taioi the net-maker's son, watched him from the coral. Tari, still a child, understood the islanders' vulnerability: war had swept away their men, leaving women, boys, and children defenseless and leaderless.  Then, yesterday, a strange boat had arrived from the north, bringing Dick and Katafa, a girl lost at sea years ago.  The women believed they were sent by the gods—a new chief, a new leader.

Tari, oblivious to the concept of gods, simply wanted answers.  He looked up at Dick, his innocent question echoing the islanders' hope. "Taori," he asked, "who are you?"

Dick couldn't answer truthfully, not in a way Tari would understand.  The name "Dick Lestrange" felt distant, a forgotten life. The language of his childhood was gone, buried under years of solitude and survival.  His past only started with Katafa, on Palm Tree Island (Marua), the memories before that blurred, swallowed by the island's sun-drenched days, storm-lashed nights, and misty rains.  Kearney, the old sailor who raised him, was almost a ghost, a faint memory, except for the little wooden ships Kearney carved as toys.  Those ships remained, tangible links to a lost world.

He looked down at the child, his own past a hazy dream. "I am Taori, Tari," he replied simply. "Why do you ask?"

"I don't know," Tari shrugged, his attention already diverted by children hauling in a net.  "I ask, just like I breathe."

Dick's gaze returned to the north. Tari’s question had stirred dormant memories: the schooner arriving at Palm Tree, only to be burned by Melanesians;  Katafa and his daring escape in Kearney's old dinghy; their arrival on this beach, the islanders' awe.  But the memory of the men he'd escaped with, their savagery, marred the beauty of the sea and sky.

He felt no immediate threat, the Palm Tree men lacked canoes. But they *would* build canoes. They would see the shimmering reflection of the Karolin lagoon in the sky, just as he had, and they would come.  It might be a long time, but they would come.

Dick was a native, a kanaka, yet the white man within him remained.  He could strategize, anticipate.  That's why he'd sent a canoe to fetch three old master canoe-builders: Aioma, Palia, and Tafata.  That's why, watching the tribe, he saw not only the nearly one hundred youths reaching fighting age, but the chance for survival, fueled by a fierce love for Katafa, a love threatened by the looming danger.

War canoes. Would he defend the lagoon, or launch a preemptive strike?  The answer was unclear, lost in the complexity of survival.

A hand touched his shoulder.  He turned to see Katafa, a stray lock of dark hair blowing free, her luminous eyes fixed on the north, the same direction as his.

"Look," she whispered.

At that moment, through the magic of mirage, Palm Tree Island appeared, seemingly drawn from the sea's heart.  First a hazy smudge, then a deepening stain, finally—vivid and ethereal—the island materialized, as breathtaking as a dream.

They stood together, their unsophisticated minds unaware of the mirage, their eyes glued to the island of their escape, now looming from afar.  They relived their terror, the monstrous figures of the Melanesians, the wind filling their tiny sail, the screams of those drunk on stolen rum.  The island’s beauty masked its dark history.

Dick’s nostrils flared, his eyes burning with hate.  They’d threatened him—that was nothing.  They’d threatened Katafa—that was everything. And they still threatened her.

They would come.  The island's reappearance confirmed his instinct. They would build canoes, see the lagoon's light, and arrive.  They craved women, and Karolin had them.

The danger wasn't just in the north, but everywhere greed, lust, and hatred lurked—among savages, and the more civilized wolves.

For Dick, the world existed only between the two islands, bounded by water.  No Europe, no America, no history but his short life—and Katafa's.  But even in this brief history, he had learned to fear men and understood the primal force that drove human conflict.

Palm Tree faded, vanished like a snuffed candle.  Katafa pointed toward a canoe approaching from the south.  It was the canoe bearing the canoe-builders.  Leaving the coral, Dick and Katafa walked towards it, their fates and the fate of Karolin hanging in the balance.
